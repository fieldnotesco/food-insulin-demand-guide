Food Insulin Demand Guide

A simple tool for estimating how much insulin your body needs to release after eating a food — a measure called Food Insulin Demand (FID).

Insulin resistance is a main driver of the condition PMOS — although not every woman with PMOS suffers with insulin resistance, it affects the vast majority. The tools on this page reflect a dietary method to manage insulin resistance based on Dr. Fiona McCulloch's book 8 Steps to Reverse Your PCOS and the underlying insulin index research from Dr. Kirstine Bell's PhD at the University of Sydney. Search for a food, add it to today's tally, or estimate the FID of something that isn't listed yet.

Fiona McCulloch considers insulin counting using the FID and Insulin Index as the single best way to regulate insulin secretion after meals and manage insulin resistance.

Estimate a Food's FID
Macro-based estimate tested Food Insulin Demand Guide
Estimate = your calories × matched food's tested FID ÷ matched food's calories.
Finds the foods in the guide whose calorie split between carb / protein / fat is closest to yours, then scales that food's tested FID to your portion's calorie count. This sidesteps the fat-blindness of the regression equation, since you're borrowing an actual measured value rather than a formula — but it's still only as good as the match. A food with a similar macro split can still behave differently (fiber, food matrix, processing all matter), so prefer a close match in food type too, not just macros.

Estimated FID
FID = calories × FII ÷ 239
FII is scaled to glucose, referenced to 239 calories (1000 kJ) of the food. This reference is compiled from published studies (Foodstruct.com) — it's a useful ballpark, but it is not the same tested methodology as the Food Insulin Demand Guide's FID values, and different studies sometimes disagree by a wide margin. Prefer the macro-based estimate above when a food is directly available there.

Estimated FID
This is the published equation from Bell, Petocz, Colagiuri & Brand-Miller (2016, Nutrients) for estimating FII from macros alone — it explains about half the variation in directly-tested FII values, using only carbohydrate and protein. Fat is deliberately left out: in their own regression, it wasn't a significant independent predictor once carb and protein were included.
